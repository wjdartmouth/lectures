# What Does "Close Reading" Mean?

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4">
<div>

### NOT close reading:
- Skimming for the general idea
- Relying on memory
- Guessing what it "probably" means

</div>
<div>

### YES close reading:
- Reading every word
- Annotating as you go
- Using context clues
- Asking "Why does the author choose *this* word?"

</div>
</div>

---

# The Three Levels of Understanding

| Level | What it means | Example |
|-------|---|---|
| **Retrieval** | Find information *directly stated* | "Holden put on a red hunting hat" |
| **Inference** | Read between the lines; what's *implied* | Holden's buying the hat = he seeks comfort after loss |
| **Interpretation** | What does it *mean in context*? Significance | The hat symbolises individuality & alienation |

# Annotation Tips

✅ **DO:**
- Underline unfamiliar words → look up meaning
- Circle repeated words → what's the pattern?
- Note shifts in tone (sad → angry → wistful)
- Write questions in margins

❌ **DON'T:**
- Over-highlight (you'll see *everything* is important)
- Assume you know a word's meaning
- Skip sentences that seem "simple"
- Annotate *after* you've finished reading

# INTERACTIVE: Inference vs. Retrieval

**Retrieval (literal):** What does the text *explicitly say*?

**Inference (reading between lines):** What can we *deduce* from clues?

### Example:
**Passage:** *"Holden's teeth were lousy and mossy. He never brushed them."*

**Retrieval Q:** What condition are Ackley's teeth in?  
**Answer:** Lousy and mossy.

**Inference Q:** What does this reveal about Ackley's character or habits?  
**Answer:** He's neglectful, unkempt, socially oblivious, or doesn't care about his appearance.

# What is a "Technique"?

A **technique** is a deliberate choice an author makes about *how to say something*.

### Examples:
- **Diction** (word choice): "foils" vs. "swords" — which word *feels* different?
- **Repetition**: Saying the same thing over → what effect?
- **Imagery**: Vivid description → helps us *feel* the scene
- **Tone**: Author's attitude (sarcastic, angry, affectionate?)
- **Symbolism**: One thing represents another (red hat = individuality)

**The key:** Don't just *name* the technique. **Always explain the effect.**

---

# The Technique Sandwich

```
[Technique name] + [Textual evidence] + [Effect on reader]
```

### ❌ Weak answer:
*"Salinger uses repetition."*

### ✅ Strong answer:
*"Salinger uses repetition of 'goddam' throughout the passage (lines 5, 32, 40, 42) 
to convey Holden's frustration and his authentic teenage voice — the repetition makes 
the reader feel his annoyance and understand his character through his speech patterns."*

---

# Paper 1: Key Techniques in *The Catcher in the Rye*

| Technique | What Salinger does | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Stream of consciousness** | Holden's thoughts jump around; digress | Intimate, reveals scattered mind |
| **Colloquial register** | "goddam," "corny," "phoney" | Authentic teenage voice |
| **Irony** | Says one thing, means another | Reveals Holden's complexity |
| **Symbolism** | Red hat, ducks, museum, carousel | Themes of innocence, loss, alienation |
| **Unreliable narration** | Holden judges others harshly | Makes reader sympathise *and* question him |

---

# Paper 2: Key Techniques in *The Swerve*

| Technique | What Greenblatt does | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Contrast** | Material goods vs. books; classical vs. medieval | Makes argument powerful and memorable |
| **Embedded quotation** | Weaves primary sources into prose | Creates authenticity and immediacy |
| **Narrative non-fiction** | Tells a story while making an argument | Engages reader emotionally + intellectually |
| **Paradox** | Contempt intensifies fame; resurrection is secular | Reveals complexity of historical moment |
| **Metaphor** | "Resurrection" for recovery of knowledge | Elevates the humanists' project |

# LESSON 3: WEDNESDAY (40 MIN)
## Paper 1 Deep Dive: *The Catcher in the Rye*

**Learning Goal:** Understand Holden's voice and key symbols

---

# Who is Holden Caulfield?

### Age: 16
### Situation: Recently expelled from Pencey Prep
### Psychology: Alienated, anxious, grieving, intelligent, contradictory
### Narrative Style: First-person; unreliable; digressive

**Key question:** Is Holden a reliable narrator?

---

# The Red Hunting Hat: Symbol Analysis

### What it IS:
- Bought impulsively in NYC
- Costs only a buck
- Worn with the peak swung to the back (unconventional)
- Red (conspicuous, not matching)

### What it REPRESENTS:
- Holden's individuality
- His non-conformity (and awareness of it)
- His alienation from social norms
- His attempt at self-expression
- Perhaps: replacement/comfort after loss

---

# Holden's Central Conflict

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4">
<div>

### What he WANTS:
- To protect innocence
- To have genuine friends
- To be accepted
- To stop time

</div>
<div>

### What he CANNOT do:
- Connect with people his own age
- Accept adult corruption
- Tolerate "phoniness"
- Move forward into adulthood

</div>
</div>

**Result:** Isolation, depression, emotional breakdown

---

# The Ducks in Central Park

### Symbol significance:
- **Where do they go in winter?** (Holden's obsessive question)
- Represents his fear of change & loss
- Shows his need for permanence
- Connects to his desire to be "catcher in the rye" (protect innocence)

### Interpretation:
The ducks' disappearance mirrors what Holden fears: displacement, abandonment, the inevitability of loss.

---

# Ackley as Foil Character

A **foil** is a character whose traits contrast with and highlight the protagonist's.

### Ackley:
- Physically awkward, poor hygiene
- Socially oblivious (doesn't read hints)
- Intrusive, boundary-violating
- Described harshly by Holden

### What this reveals about HOLDEN:
- He's judgmental and harsh
- He *craves* privacy and respect
- His contempt masks his own loneliness
- He struggles to connect despite (or because of) his intelligence

---

# INTERACTIVE: Essay Planning (20 min)

**Essay Prompt:** *"How does Salinger use Holden's narrative voice to generate both sympathy and frustration in the reader?"*

### Steps:
1. **Identify 3 techniques** Salinger uses (e.g., sarcasm, stream of consciousness, unreliability)
2. **Find 1 quote** for each technique
3. **Explain effect:** How does it make us sympathize? Frustrate us?

**Example start:**
- Salinger's use of colloquial language ("goddam," "corny") makes Holden sound authentic and relatable (sympathy)...
- BUT his harsh judgments of Ackley reveal his cruelty and isolation (frustration)...

---

- **Mental health:** Depression, anxiety, breakdown

**Learning Goal:** Understand Greenblatt's argument and craft

---

# What is *The Swerve* About?

**Central argument:** The rediscovery of Lucretius's *De rerum natura* (On the Nature of Things) by Renaissance humanist Poggio Bracciolini changed the world.

### Why does it matter?
- Lucretius = ancient Roman, wrote about atomism, pleasure, absence of fear
- Medieval Church = suppressed these "dangerous" pagan ideas
- Humanists like Poggio = recovered classical texts, sparked Renaissance
- Result: The modern world (secularism, individualism, reason)

---

# Petrarch: The Obsessed Scholar

### Who he was:
- 14th-century Italian humanist
- Devout Christian BUT fascinated by pagan antiquity (the paradox!)
- Wrote poetry, philosophy, letters
- Obsessively collected and copied ancient manuscripts

### His contribution:
- Showed that the past was *genuinely lost* (not continuous with medieval era)
- Made recovery of classical texts an intellectual *passion*, not just scholarship

---

# The Paradox at the Heart of Chapter 5

**Petrarch's contradiction:**
- Intensely religious (took religious vows)
- Intensely obsessed with pagan classical culture
- Lived in contempt for his own era
- Yet his disdain made him MORE famous

**Greenblatt's point:** This contradiction is NOT a weakness — it's the *engine* of the Renaissance. The humanists could move forward because they insisted the past was genuinely different.

---

# Greenblatt's Key Rhetorical Moves

### 1. The Books Quotation (Lines 10–13)
**Technique:** Contrast (material goods vs. spiritual nourishment)

**Effect:** Makes us understand why recovering *books* mattered more than recovering jewels or statues.

### 2. "Manic Energy" (Line 16)
**Technique:** Word choice

**Effect:** Conveys the *obsessive passion* of the work, not mere scholarship.

### 3. "Resurrection" (Later in chapter)
**Technique:** Metaphor

**Effect:** Elevates cultural recovery to the level of spiritual transformation.

---

# Lucretius & Epicureanism

### What Lucretius argued:
- The universe = atoms moving in void
- No divine intervention
- Pleasure (freedom from pain/fear) = highest good
- Death is nothing to fear

### Why the Church feared this:
- Contradicts Christian cosmology
- Challenges religious authority
- Suggests life is about *this world*, not salvation
- Offers alternative worldview to Christianity

**Greenblatt's argument:** This book was *dangerous knowledge* — and that's why its recovery mattered so much.

---

# INTERACTIVE: Argument Analysis (20 min)

**Question:** How does Greenblatt build credibility (ethos) as a historian in Chapter 5?

### Consider:
1. Does he cite sources? How?
2. Does he acknowledge complexity? (e.g., "Petrarch was not the first...")
3. Does he blend narrative with analysis?
4. What's his tone?

**Task:** Find ONE sentence that shows Greenblatt being both scholarly AND engaging, and explain why.

---

# Essay Planning for Paper 2 (15 min)

**Essay Prompt:** *"How does Greenblatt make The Swerve both scholarly and compelling? Use examples from Chapters 1–7."*

### Structure:
1. **Thesis:** Greenblatt blends [narrative + analysis / storytelling + evidence / character + ideas]
2. **Evidence 1:** Petrarch's characterisation
3. **Evidence 2:** The contrast about books
4. **Evidence 3:** Larger historical argument
5. **Conclusion:** Why does this blend matter?

---

# Key Themes for Paper 2 Essay

- **The power of ideas:** How dangerous knowledge shapes history
- **Individual agency:** How one person (Poggio) can matter historically
- **The fragility of culture:** Books decay, knowledge is lost
- **Classical vs. medieval worldviews:** The swerve from one to the other
- **Craft & scholarship:** How a historian tells a story while arguing

# Common Mistakes to AVOID: Sections A & B

❌ **Section A:**
- Answering in your own words when evidence is needed
- Not reading the whole passage before answering
- Misquoting or misremembering details

❌ **Section B:**
- Just naming techniques without explaining effects
- Using weak or vague language ("This shows...")
- Forgetting to connect technique to character/theme

---

# Common Mistakes to AVOID: Section C

❌ **Section C:**
- No clear thesis
- Plot summary instead of analysis
- Quotes that don't support your argument

---

# The "PEE" Paragraph: Your Secret Weapon

**Point → Evidence → Explanation**

```
[Topic sentence/Point]
[Quotation/Evidence from text]
[Explanation: Why does this matter? 
How does it support your argument?]
```

### Example:
**Point:** Holden's use of colloquial language reveals his authentic teenage voice.
**Evidence:** He repeatedly uses words like "goddam" and "corny."
**Explanation:** This makes the reader trust him and sympathise with his isolation, even as his judgment of others frustrates us.

# Quick Wins: Last 10 Minutes

### If you're panicking:
1. **Breathe.** You've prepared.
2. **Reread the question.** Make sure you're answering what's asked.
3. **Check for quoted passages.** Use them as anchors for your evidence.
4. **Read your essay out loud (silently).** Does it make sense?

### If you're running out of time:
- Finish your essay with a clear conclusion, even if it's brief
- Better to have a shorter complete answer than an unfinished one
- Bullet points are OK if you need to list ideas quickly

# Final Thoughts

### What makes a STRONG exam answer:
1. **Close, careful reading** of the passage
2. **Specific evidence** (quotes, line references)
3. **Clear explanation** of effects and significance
4. **Your own voice** (paraphrase, don't just copy)
5. **Structured argument** (especially in essays)

### What makes a WEAK exam answer:
- General statements without evidence
- Naming techniques without explaining effects
- Plot summary instead of analysis
- Vague language ("This is important because...")
